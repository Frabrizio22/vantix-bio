Batch Testing: Why Every Batch Matters

April 10, 2026 · 8 min read Testing Standards

A Certificate of Analysis proves what was in the vial that was tested. It doesn't prove what's in the vial you received—unless both came from the same batch.

Batch-level testing is the foundation of research reproducibility. When suppliers test at the brand level (one COA for multiple production runs) rather than the batch level (unique verification for each batch), they introduce a gap between claimed quality and delivered quality.

This article explains why batch-specific verification matters, what causes variation between batches, and how to verify the specific material in your laboratory.

What Is a Batch?

A batch (also called a lot) is a quantity of material produced in a single manufacturing cycle under uniform conditions. In peptide synthesis, a batch typically represents:

  • One synthesis run using the same reagent lots
  • One purification sequence
  • One lyophilization cycle
  • Material filled into vials on the same day

Batch identifiers (e.g., "VX-BPC10-001") link a physical vial to its production record and analytical certificate. Without this traceability, there's no way to verify that testing data corresponds to received material.

Why Batches Vary

Synthesis Variability

Solid-phase peptide synthesis (SPPS) depends on sequential coupling reactions. Each amino acid addition represents an opportunity for incomplete reaction, side products, or deletion sequences. Small variations in reagent quality, coupling time, or temperature affect final purity.

Research on therapeutic peptide manufacturing has documented batch-to-batch purity variation of 2-5% even under Good Manufacturing Practice conditions.1 For research-grade material produced without pharmaceutical controls, variation can exceed 10%.

Example: Three consecutive batches of the same peptide tested at an independent laboratory:
  • Batch A: 98.2% purity (HPLC)
  • Batch B: 96.8% purity (HPLC)
  • Batch C: 97.5% purity (HPLC)

All three batches meet a "≥95% purity" specification, but Batch A contains 44% more impurities than Batch B. If your research was performed with Batch A and a colleague's with Batch C, you're not working with identical material.

Raw Material Drift

Peptide manufacturers source protected amino acids, coupling reagents, and resins from chemical suppliers. When a supplier changes lots or sources, synthesis outcomes can shift even when the protocol remains constant.

A study of insulin analog production found that switching resin suppliers introduced a 0.3% difference in final purity and altered the impurity profile despite identical synthesis conditions.2 For complex sequences like TB-500 (43 amino acids), small changes compound across the synthesis.

Purification Variables

HPLC purification separates the target peptide from synthesis byproducts, but column performance degrades with use. The first batch purified on a fresh column may achieve higher purity than the 50th batch on the same column.

Process drift in chromatography has been documented to cause 1-3% purity variation over a column's lifecycle, even when operators follow standard procedures.3

Storage and Handling

Peptides degrade over time through oxidation, deamidation, and aggregation. Even when stored properly (frozen, desiccated), degradation rates vary based on sequence composition.

Peptides containing methionine, cysteine, or asparagine residues are particularly susceptible to oxidation and deamidation during storage.4 A COA generated at the time of synthesis doesn't account for degradation during warehousing or shipping.

The Problem with Brand-Level Testing

Some suppliers test one batch per product and present that COA as representative of all future batches. This practice creates several problems:

1. No Verification of Current Material

If you receive Batch 047 but the COA is from Batch 001 (produced six months earlier), you have no analytical verification of what you actually received. The manufacturer's process may have drifted. The raw materials may have changed. The COA data is historical, not current.

2. Cherry-Picking Risk

When suppliers test once and apply that result to multiple batches, there's incentive to test only the best batch. Batch 001 might test at 98.5% purity, but Batches 005, 012, and 023 were never tested. If you receive one of the untested batches, you're working blind.

3. No Traceability

Without batch-specific testing, there's no way to investigate variability in research results. If an experiment fails to replicate, you can't determine whether it's due to protocol differences or material differences—because you don't know if the material was actually the same.

What Batch Testing Provides

Material-Specific Verification

When every batch is tested, the COA describes the actual material you received. You can verify purity, identity, and contaminant levels for your specific vial, not for a representative sample from months earlier.

Process Monitoring

Batch-to-batch testing reveals manufacturing consistency. If purity varies by 5% across batches, that signals process instability. If endotoxin levels spike in one batch, that identifies a contamination event. Without per-batch data, these issues stay hidden until they cause experimental failures.

Research Reproducibility

When you document which batch was used in each experiment, collaborators can request the same batch (if available) or compare batch analytics to understand why results differ. This is standard practice in pharmaceutical development but rare in research peptide supply.

How to Verify Your Batch

1. Check for Batch Identifiers

Your vial should have a batch number or lot code. This identifier must match the COA. If the vial says "Batch 023" but the COA is for "Batch 001," the COA doesn't verify your material.

2. Verify Task ID Correspondence

If the supplier uses third-party testing (e.g., Janoshik Analytical), the COA will include a Task ID. You can verify that Task ID on the testing laboratory's website. Check that:

  • The Task ID is real (not fabricated)
  • The date makes sense (COA should be recent if the batch is new)
  • The client name matches your supplier (or is listed as "confidential")

See our guide on How to Read a Janoshik COA for details on Task ID verification.

3. Look for Multiple Test Methods

Comprehensive batch testing includes:

  • HPLC purity: Quantifies the target peptide and impurities
  • Mass spectrometry (LC-MS or MALDI): Confirms molecular identity
  • Endotoxin testing (LAL assay): Detects bacterial contamination

A COA showing only one test method (e.g., purity alone) leaves gaps. Identity confirmation is essential—purity doesn't prove you received the right peptide. Endotoxin testing matters if the material will be used in cell culture or biological assays. Learn more in our Endotoxin Testing Deep Dive.

4. Assess Testing Recency

Check the test date on the COA. If the COA is dated 18 months before you received the vial, the material has been sitting in storage for over a year. Degradation may have occurred. Recent testing (within 3-6 months of receipt) is more reliable.

Industry Standards vs. Research Reality

In pharmaceutical manufacturing, batch testing is mandatory. Every production lot undergoes release testing before distribution. The FDA requires full analytical verification of each batch of active pharmaceutical ingredients.5

Research peptide suppliers operate under different rules. There's no regulatory requirement for batch-specific testing, so practices vary widely:

  • High-end suppliers: Test every batch with HPLC + MS + endotoxin, provide batch-matched COAs
  • Mid-tier suppliers: Test representative batches, may reuse COAs across multiple batches
  • Budget suppliers: Provide manufacturer's COA (client name is the raw material supplier, not your vendor), no independent verification

The price difference between these tiers often reflects testing costs more than material costs.

The Cost of Batch Testing

Full analytical testing (HPLC + MS + endotoxin) costs approximately $200-400 per batch. For a supplier producing 20-vial batches of a $40 product, that's $10-20 per vial in testing overhead.

Some suppliers skip batch testing to keep prices low. Others test representative batches and spread the cost across multiple production runs. A few test every batch and build the cost into pricing.

The question isn't whether batch testing is worth it in absolute terms—it clearly improves quality assurance. The question is whether it's worth it for your research. If you're performing preliminary screens or protocol optimization, representative testing may suffice. If you're generating data for publication or need to replicate prior work exactly, batch-specific verification becomes essential.

Recommendations for Researchers

  1. Record batch numbers: Document the batch ID for every peptide used in experiments. This enables troubleshooting if results don't replicate.
  2. Request batch-matched COAs: Don't accept generic COAs that don't match your received material.
  3. Verify independently when possible: If research outcomes are critical, consider having material independently tested rather than relying solely on supplier COAs.
  4. Compare batches: If you're using a new batch of a previously characterized peptide, compare COAs to assess consistency.

Key Takeaways

  • Batch-to-batch variation is inherent in peptide synthesis—even well-controlled processes show 2-5% purity differences
  • Brand-level testing (one COA for all batches) doesn't verify the specific material you received
  • Batch identifiers enable traceability between physical vials and analytical certificates
  • Comprehensive batch testing includes purity (HPLC), identity (MS), and endotoxin verification
  • Research reproducibility depends on knowing whether material differences contributed to outcome differences
