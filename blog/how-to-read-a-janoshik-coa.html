How to Read a Janoshik COA: A Section‑by‑Section Breakdown

April 8, 2026 9 min read Quality Verification

Janoshik Analytical is the most widely recognized independent testing laboratory in the research peptide community. When a vendor publishes a Janoshik COA, they're providing third-party verification that didn't come from their own facility or their manufacturer's QC lab.

But a COA is only useful if you can read it. Most buyers glance at the purity number, see something above 98%, and move on. The report contains significantly more information than that — and understanding each section tells you not just whether the product is pure, but whether it's the right molecule, whether it's biologically clean, and whether the report actually belongs to the batch you purchased.

This guide walks through each section of a Janoshik peptide COA, explains what the data means, and shows you how to verify the report independently.

Research Use Only: All analytical methods and compounds discussed in this article pertain to preclinical research materials. This content is educational and does not constitute medical advice.

Section 1: The Report Header

Every Janoshik COA begins with an identification block at the top of the document. This section establishes the chain of custody between the sample and the report.

Header Fields

Task ID Unique identifier for this specific test submission
Sample Name Compound name as provided by the submitter
Client / Vendor The individual or company that submitted the sample
Date When the analysis was completed

The Task ID is the most important field on the entire report. It's the unique key that ties this specific PDF to Janoshik's internal database. Without it, the document is unverifiable — it could be copied from another test, edited in Photoshop, or fabricated entirely.

A COA without a Task ID is not a Janoshik COA. It's a PDF.

What to check:

  • Task ID present and legible — This is your verification key. Write it down.
  • Sample name matches what you ordered — If you purchased BPC-157 5mg and the COA says "BPC-157 10mg," it may not correspond to your product.
  • Date is recent — A COA from 18 months ago may not reflect the current batch. Peptides degrade over time, and a fresh test on a fresh batch is always more relevant than an archived one.
  • Client name matches the vendor — If the vendor claims they submitted the sample, their name (or company name) should appear on the report. If it shows a different submitter, the vendor may be republishing someone else's test.

Section 2: HPLC Purity Analysis

This is the section most buyers focus on — and it's also the section most frequently misunderstood. Janoshik performs reversed-phase HPLC, typically with UV detection at 220 nm, which is a standard wavelength for peptide bond absorption [1].

The Chromatogram

The chromatogram is the graph — a plot of UV absorbance (y-axis) against retention time in minutes (x-axis). Each peak represents a compound that absorbed UV light as it eluted from the HPLC column. The tall, dominant peak is your target peptide. Any smaller peaks flanking it are impurities.

What the chromatogram tells you:

  • Main peak retention time — The time (in minutes) at which the target compound eluted from the column. This varies by peptide and method conditions, but it should be consistent across runs of the same compound under the same method.
  • Peak shape — A sharp, symmetrical peak indicates a well-resolved, homogeneous compound. Tailing, shouldering, or broadening can indicate co-elution with impurities, column degradation, or an overloaded sample.
  • Baseline noise — A clean, flat baseline between peaks indicates good method performance. Elevated baseline or drift may complicate accurate integration of minor impurities.

The Purity Number

Below or beside the chromatogram, Janoshik reports the purity as a percentage — typically labeled something like "Purity (HPLC): 98.7%." This number represents the area of the main peak divided by the total area of all detected peaks, expressed as a percentage.

What HPLC Purity Does and Doesn't Tell You

  • Does tell you: What proportion of UV-absorbing material is the target compound
  • Does tell you: Whether synthesis impurities (deletions, truncations) are present
  • Doesn't tell you: Whether the main peak is actually the correct molecule
  • Doesn't tell you: What percentage of the vial weight is active peptide vs. counterion salt
  • Doesn't tell you: Whether the sample contains endotoxins or biological contaminants

HPLC purity is necessary but not sufficient. It answers the question "how pure is this?" — not "what is this?" That's what the next section is for. For a deeper look at what HPLC purity misses, see What Your COA Isn't Telling You.

Method details to look for:

Some Janoshik reports include method parameters — column type, mobile phase composition, gradient conditions, and detection wavelength. When present, these details allow a researcher to evaluate whether the analytical method was appropriate for the specific peptide. Reversed-phase C18 columns with acetonitrile/water gradients containing 0.1% TFA are standard for most synthetic peptides [2]. If method details are absent, the purity number is still meaningful — it just can't be independently replicated.

Section 3: Mass Spectrometry — Identity Confirmation

If HPLC tells you how pure, mass spectrometry tells you what it is. This is the section that separates a complete COA from a partial one.

Janoshik typically runs LC-MS (liquid chromatography–mass spectrometry) or standalone MS, measuring the mass-to-charge ratio (m/z) of the compound. The report shows:

Mass Spectrometry Data Points

Expected MW Theoretical molecular weight of the target peptide (from its amino acid sequence)
Observed MW Molecular weight measured by the instrument
Mass Spectrum Graph showing m/z peaks — the molecular "fingerprint"
Charge States Multiple m/z peaks from the same molecule carrying different charges ([M+2H]²⁺, [M+3H]³⁺, etc.)

How to interpret it:

Match between expected and observed MW — This is the critical comparison. If the expected molecular weight of BPC-157 is 1419.53 Da and the observed mass is 1419.5 Da, the identity is confirmed. A mismatch of more than ~1 Da (for standard MS) or ~0.01 Da (for high-resolution MS) warrants investigation.

Charge state envelope — Larger peptides produce multiple peaks in the mass spectrum, each representing the same molecule with a different number of proton charges. Seeing a consistent charge state envelope (e.g., [M+2H]²⁺ at 710.3, [M+3H]³⁺ at 473.8 for a 1419 Da peptide) is a strong confirmation of identity. These peaks should form a mathematically consistent series — if they don't, the sample may contain multiple compounds.

Why this matters: Two peptides with similar amino acid compositions can produce nearly identical HPLC retention times. A truncated synthesis product missing a terminal amino acid might show 99% HPLC purity but the wrong molecular weight. Mass spectrometry catches what HPLC cannot — it confirms the compound is actually the molecule you ordered [3].

Section 4: Endotoxin Testing (When Included)

Not every Janoshik COA includes this section. Endotoxin testing via the LAL (Limulus Amebocyte Lysate) assay is an add-on service that vendors must specifically request and pay for. Its presence on a COA means the vendor invested in biological safety testing beyond the standard chemical panel.

When included, the endotoxin section reports:

  • Result in EU/mL — Endotoxin Units per milliliter. This is the quantitative measurement. Lower is better.
  • Detection method — Typically kinetic chromogenic LAL or gel-clot LAL, both derived from the horseshoe crab clotting cascade first described by Levin and Bang [4].
  • Detection limit — The lowest concentration the assay can reliably measure (commonly 0.005–0.05 EU/mL depending on the method sensitivity).

What the numbers mean:

The USP <85> bacterial endotoxin test establishes the framework for LAL testing of pharmaceutical products [5]. While there is no official regulatory limit for research-use peptides specifically, the pharmaceutical threshold for parenteral drugs provides useful context: the FDA's general limit for injectable products is 5 EU/kg body weight per hour.

For research purposes, a result reported as <0.1 EU/mL or similar low values indicates the sample carries minimal endotoxin burden. (For a full breakdown of LAL methodology and why it matters, see our Endotoxin Testing Deep Dive.) Results above 1.0 EU/mL may be worth noting, particularly for research involving immune pathways, cell viability assays, or any model where LPS-driven TLR4 activation could confound results.

Key point: If the endotoxin section is absent from a Janoshik COA, it means the test was not ordered — not that the product passed or failed. The absence of data is not the same as a negative result.

Section 5: Verification — Confirming the Report Is Real

This is the step most buyers skip, and it's arguably the most important one. A Janoshik COA is only as trustworthy as your ability to verify it independently.

How to verify:

  1. Locate the Task ID on the COA header (top of the first page).
  2. Go to janoshik.com/verification.
  3. Enter the Task ID into the verification form.
  4. Compare the returned information against the PDF the vendor provided — compound name, test date, and results should match.

If the Task ID doesn't return a result, or the returned data doesn't match the PDF, the document may be fabricated, edited, or recycled from a different submission.

Why verification matters:

A PDF is a file. Anyone with basic software can edit text, change purity numbers, swap compound names, or paste a Janoshik logo onto a manufacturer's in-house report. The verification portal is Janoshik's defense against this — it creates a link between the physical document and the laboratory's internal records that can't be forged without compromising Janoshik's own systems.

If a vendor's COA verifies, you know:

  • Janoshik actually tested a sample under that Task ID
  • The results on the PDF match what Janoshik has on file
  • The document hasn't been modified after issuance

If a vendor's COA does not verify, you know nothing. Treat unverifiable COAs with appropriate skepticism.

Red Flags: What to Watch For

Not every COA with a Janoshik logo is a reliable indicator of product quality. Here are patterns that warrant closer scrutiny:

COA Red Flags

No Task ID Cannot be verified → treat as unverifiable claim
Old Date COA from 12+ months ago may not reflect current batch
Different Client Vendor name ≠ client on COA → may be republishing someone else's test
HPLC Only No mass spec = purity confirmed, identity not confirmed
Reused Across Batches Same COA for every order = not batch-specific testing
Unusually Round Numbers "99.0%" exactly is less common than "98.7%" or "99.2%" in real analytical data

None of these individually prove a problem. A vendor may legitimately reuse a COA if they're selling from a single large batch. An older test date doesn't mean the peptide has degraded. But multiple red flags on the same report should prompt additional questions.

Putting It All Together

A complete Janoshik COA with HPLC purity, mass spectrometry identification, and endotoxin testing answers three distinct questions:

  1. How pure is this? → HPLC chromatogram and purity percentage
  2. Is this the right molecule? → Mass spectrum with expected vs. observed molecular weight
  3. Is this biologically clean? → LAL endotoxin result in EU/mL

Each test addresses a different category of risk. HPLC catches synthesis impurities. MS catches misidentification. LAL catches biological contamination. Together, they provide a significantly more complete picture than any single test alone.

The verification step — checking the Task ID on janoshik.com — adds a fourth dimension: authenticity. It confirms the data is real, unaltered, and traceable to a specific laboratory submission.

When evaluating a vendor's Janoshik COA, ask four questions in order:

  1. Does the Task ID verify on janoshik.com?
  2. Does the report include both HPLC purity and mass spectrometry identity confirmation?
  3. Is the test date recent enough to correspond to the batch I'm purchasing?
  4. Is endotoxin data included — and if not, why not?

The answers won't always be perfect. Not every vendor tests every batch. Not every COA includes endotoxin. But knowing what to look for — and what's missing — puts you in a fundamentally stronger position than accepting a purity number at face value.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a Janoshik Task ID and how do I use it?

A Task ID is a unique identifier assigned to each test submission. It appears in the COA header. Enter it at janoshik.com/verification to confirm the report is authentic. If the ID doesn't verify, the document may not be genuine.

What's a good HPLC purity for research peptides?

Above 95% is generally considered acceptable for research-grade synthetic peptides, with most reputable vendors targeting 98% or higher. Purity below 95% may indicate incomplete synthesis or degradation. But always evaluate purity alongside identity confirmation — a high number only matters if the compound is the right molecule.

Does every Janoshik COA include endotoxin testing?

No. Endotoxin testing is an add-on that vendors must specifically request and pay for. Standard Janoshik testing covers HPLC purity and mass spectrometry. If the endotoxin section is absent, it means the test wasn't ordered — not that the product passed.

Can I verify a COA if I'm not the one who submitted the sample?

Yes. Janoshik's verification portal is public — anyone with the Task ID can check whether a report is authentic. This is by design: it allows end customers to independently verify vendor claims without relying on the vendor's word.

What if the observed molecular weight doesn't exactly match the expected value?

Small deviations are normal. Standard mass spectrometers have measurement tolerances — a difference of ±0.5 Da on a standard instrument or ±0.01 Da on a high-resolution instrument is within expected range. Larger deviations (>1 Da) suggest the compound may not be the target peptide, or may contain significant modifications or adducts that require investigation.

Should I be concerned if a vendor reuses the same COA for multiple orders?

It depends. If the vendor is selling from one batch and the COA corresponds to that batch, reuse is legitimate. The concern arises when a vendor uses the same COA across batches synthesized at different times — the report no longer reflects the specific product you received. Ask whether the COA matches your batch, and verify the Task ID.
