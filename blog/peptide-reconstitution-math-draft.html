Peptide Reconstitution Calculator: Complete Mathematical Guide

Published: April 14, 2026 · 6 min read

Accurate peptide reconstitution is fundamental to reproducible research. A single calculation error can invalidate an entire study—wrong concentrations lead to incorrect dosing, inconsistent results, and wasted materials.

This guide provides the mathematical framework for peptide reconstitution with appropriate research diluent, including reference tables for common vial sizes.

The Core Formula

All peptide reconstitution calculations use the same basic equation:

Concentration Formula
Concentration (mg/ml) = Peptide Mass (mg) ÷ Water Volume (ml)

Example: A 10mg BPC-157 vial reconstituted with 2ml appropriate research diluent yields 5 mg/ml concentration.

This concentration tells you how much peptide is in each milliliter of solution—critical for calculating specific research doses.

Choosing Reconstitution Volume

The "right" volume depends on your research protocol:

Lower volume (1-2ml):

  • Higher concentration = smaller injection volumes
  • Useful when research model has volume limitations
  • Requires more precise measurement (errors magnified)

Higher volume (3-5ml):

  • Lower concentration = easier to measure accurately
  • Reduces dosing errors from syringe tolerance
  • May exceed vial capacity or create storage challenges

Standard Practice: Most research protocols use 2ml reconstitution for 10mg vials (5 mg/ml) as a balance between concentration and measurement accuracy.

Syringe Unit Conversions

Research syringes are marked in different units. Converting between them prevents errors:

Common syringe markings:

  • Milliliters (ml): Standard volume unit (0.1ml, 0.5ml, 1ml)
  • Cubic centimeters (cc): 1 cc = 1 ml (identical)
  • Units: Typically 100 units = 1ml on insulin syringes

Quick conversion:

  • 0.1ml = 10 units (on 100-unit syringe)
  • 0.5ml = 50 units
  • 1.0ml = 100 units

For a 5 mg/ml solution, drawing 0.2ml (20 units on insulin syringe) delivers 1mg of peptide.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. Confusing peptide mass with concentration

A 10mg vial does NOT automatically give you 10mg/ml. Concentration depends on how much water you add.

2. Ignoring overfill

Many vials contain slight overfill (10-15%). Calculate based on labeled mass, not actual content, for consistency with literature protocols.

3. Using wrong syringe type

1ml insulin syringes (100 units) are NOT the same as 1ml luer-lock syringes (no unit markings). Know your syringe scale.

4. Skipping verification math

Always double-check: (desired dose in mg) ÷ (concentration in mg/ml) = volume to draw in ml.

Calculation Check: If a protocol calls for 0.5mg and your concentration is 2.5 mg/ml, you need 0.2ml (20 units). Drawing 0.5ml would deliver 1.25mg—a 2.5× overdose.

Storage After Reconstitution

Once reconstituted, peptides have limited stability:

  • Refrigerated (2-8°C): Most peptides stable 14-28 days
  • Room temperature: Degradation accelerates, use within hours
  • Frozen (-20°C): Extends shelf life but avoid repeated freeze-thaw cycles

For protocols exceeding 28 days, consider aliquoting reconstituted material into single-use portions and freezing.

See: Peptide Storage Guide for detailed preservation protocols.

Verification Before Use

Before beginning research protocols, verify reconstitution success:

  • Visual inspection: Solution should be clear, no visible particles or cloudiness
  • Dissolution time: Most peptides dissolve within 1-2 minutes of gentle swirling
  • Volume check: Confirm total volume matches expected (peptide powder adds negligible volume)

Cloudy solutions, slow dissolution, or visible aggregates suggest degradation or contamination. Do not use questionable material.
